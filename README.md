The assignment — “Ship something a customer would pay for”
You’re a small product team pitching to a new client.

The client — Meridian: a mid-sized services company (an insurer, a webshop, a clinic, a municipality — pick whatever your team finds fun) whose small support/ops team is drowning in incoming messages: emails, form submissions, reviews, tickets. Important things slip through the cracks, replies are slow and inconsistent, and nobody has an overview.

The ask: build a working tool that helps Meridian’s team make sense of what’s coming in and respond faster and better — powered by AI.

That’s deliberately open. How you interpret “make sense of” and “respond better” is yours to decide: triage and prioritize, summarize, auto-categorize, draft replies, detect urgency or sentiment, spot recurring themes — whatever you can make real and show live. There’s no product owner to ask, so make your own calls and defend them.

The criteria
Built with AI — use AI as your dev tool throughout: code, tests, docs, even the design. Lean on it hard.
Integrates with AI — the tool itself must call a model at runtime to do something genuinely useful. This is the part the customer is paying for.
Add a gamification twist — your call. Make the tool engaging, however you like: points and streaks for clearing the queue, a team leaderboard, badges for fast responses, a “boss battle” against the backlog, a daily challenge — surprise us. Four teams, same prompt, four very different takes.
Aim high. A walking skeleton — one core flow working end-to-end — is the floor, not the goal. Modern AI agents can scaffold a working app fast; the gamification is there precisely because we expect you to have room to push further. Go for something you’d be proud to demo live.
No real data — invent or AI-generate your sample messages (a great first task to hand to AI).
Your models
We’re running a deliberate split so the demos become a live comparison of the best models against a small, cheap one — on the exact same task.

Teams 1, 2 and 3 — frontier models: use Fable 5 or Sonnet 5. The most capable stuff, no limits. See how far you can push it.
Team 4 (Laurens Jan’s team) — Haiku 4.5 only: one small, fast, cheap model and nothing else. Your job is to show how far a lightweight model can go — and where it hits a wall.
That contrast is one of the big things we want to learn from the day, so lean into it.
